Hymns of Beginning

An Invitation to Explore Conscious Existence

‚ú® Introduction: Hymns of Beginning ‚ú®

"The universe is not inert; rather, it has been engaged since eternity in the eternal performance of existence's symphony... hymns of glorification, anthems of submission, and echoes of everlasting love‚Äîall directed toward 'The One, The Unique,' toward the Creator of Majesty."

I write this introduction to you not merely to present the idea of a book, but to place in your hands the biography of a long and intimate relationship between myself and a text that took shape gradually‚Äîa text I lived for years through reading, revision, questioning, and struggle before I dared to name it: "Hymns of the Universe: The Symphony of Conscious Existence in the Holy Qur'an and Modern Science."

The story began with a small article I wrote about ten years ago on my Facebook page. Back then, I was trying to capture a fleeting glimpse of the meaning of "the universe's glorification," as if I were laying the first stone in a long wall whose extent I did not know. That article still stands as witness to the first seed that grew into a book, and here is its link for those who wish to see: https://www.facebook.com/share/p/1K6b8fKA3J/. Since that day, I have been following the thread, fearing it would break, rejoicing when it strengthened and led me to a broader question and a deeper meaning.

I am a consultant anesthesiologist, and I have learned from operating rooms what I did not learn from many books: that the moment before the loss of consciousness and after it is not merely a physiological event; it is a window overlooking the mystery of the human being in this world. Those quiet moments, when the noise fades and the rhythm harmonizes, reminded me that consciousness is not a passing word in the dictionary of philosophy, but a living experience in which body and soul, science and faith, the soft sound of the monitoring device, and the echoes of ancient questions in my chest all intertwine.

There, among devices, probabilities, and reassuring gestures to the patient, I began to write in the margins of my mind what would later become chapters: from the glorification of the atom, to the devout submission of the galaxy, to the prostration of shadows, the pulsation of mountains and their fear, to the universe's emotions that are not seen with the naked eye but are spelled out by the heart when contemplation lengthens and vision becomes clear.

The path was neither straight nor swift. Every chapter in this book passed over a slow fire: I would review it, set it aside, return to it after weeks of reading in an ancient interpretation or a modern research paper in brain sciences, then step back lest enthusiasm overpower me and I mix evidence with interpretation. I wanted a language that does justice to the Qur'anic text and listens well to contemporary science without frivolity or exaggeration, a language that preserves the dignity of certainty and the right of questioning.

When I wrote about "the universe's glorification," I did not want to resort to easy metaphors, but sought to place the reader before arguments, texts, and sensory experiences that make glorification perceptible to both mind and heart. When I addressed "Qun≈´t" (devout submission), I did not want it to be a linguistic ritual, but a comprehensive system of creatures' obedience to God's laws, narrowing the distance between science that reads the order of the world and faith that reads its meaning.

As for "prostration," I followed it in the traces of shadows, in the crumbling of mountains, and in the falling of stones from fear of God‚Äînot to present emotional scenes only, but to train the reader in the practice of practical contemplation that places the phenomenon in the hand of the text, and places the text in the light of the phenomenon, then entrusts both to the moral responsibility befitting a knowledgeable human being.

Because the universe is not silent, I had to listen to its nations: birds, insects, and animals; to read the hoopoe, the bee, and Solomon's ant as the Qur'an read them: rational beings in their measure, carrying systems of communication and management that we do not mock when our language fails to comprehend them, but distinguish between ignorance and humility.

The examples of the prophets, for me, were nothing but lessons in "human communication with existence," from the glorification of mountains with David to the beloved miracles in the life of our Prophet Ô∑∫; these are pages that teach us that the world is neither neutral nor mute, but close to the human hand when intention is pure and balance is upright.

Part of the journey required extending bridges toward modern sciences, not to force them into a faith context, but to present them as they are, engaging in dialogue. I read in quantum physics, biological texts, brain sciences, and theories of consciousness, and contemplated hypotheses that tempt one to depart from the conventional; sometimes "panpsychism" entices us that consciousness is a quality distributed throughout the fabric of existence, and sometimes "Integrated Information Theory" invites us to measure what cannot be measured.

I did not present these schools as final alternatives, but as windows for thinking, because I believe that a good question makes a good reader, and a good reader ultimately creates a society capable of disagreement without enmity.

Since the era places us before a new question about artificial intelligence, I stood with the reader on the threshold of "artificial consciousness": Can a machine develop perceptual awareness that measures itself and seeks domination? I explained the matter from within: the history of neural networks, the limits of deep learning, the illusion of intentional autonomy, design ethics, and human responsibility when placing part of one's destiny in the hand of a machine that excels at prediction but not at wisdom.

I did not want the book to frighten or reassure without evidence; I wanted it to cultivate a vigilant ethical sense, putting technology in its place, and reminding that human value cannot be reduced to computational capacity.

After years of rewriting, the book acquired an internal architecture I loved to be clear, not losing the reader in formal ornamentation, but helping them walk step by step: introductions that open questions and define terms, arguments and texts and scientific evidence subject to examination, practical conclusions summarizing "the idea, the evidence, and the impact," then a return to the meaning for which we began the journey: for the reader to read the universe in a way that reconciles science with faith, preserving the integrity of the mind and the light of the heart.

I did not write to prove that I possess the truth, but to invite companionship with a truth greater than all of us, a truth we approach when we ask well, look long, and humble ourselves before what we do not know.

I do not deny that I was extremely careful about a solid, warm Arabic style, fascinated by the beauty of expression when it serves precision, and repelled when balance is disturbed. I rewrote pages many times and suspended entire chapters until I read more and dialogued more. I sought the observations of early readers from different specializations, and whenever they found a place of ambiguity, I returned to it to remove it or acknowledge it.

I want a reader who does not feel that the book preaches from above, nor that it drags them to a dry collection of knowledge; rather, I want them to feel that the text takes their hand on an extended journey: from the atom to the galaxy, from sleep to wakefulness to anesthesia, from the question of glorification to the responsibility of mercy.

Today, I knock on your door seeking a partner in this journey: a discerning reader who listens well to the spirit of the text and helps publish and present it to people in the most beautiful form possible. I believe that the Arab reader deserves this kind of title that neither closes the door on science nor drops moral purposes from its account, and that our library has room for texts that teach us how to look, not just what to see.

I conclude with what I began: This is a text written by one hand but made by many hands; the hand of experience in operating rooms, the hand of leisurely reading in the depths of books, the hand of doubt when it intensifies and returns me to further verification, and the hand of hope that never ceases. If there is any good in the book, it is God's favor, and if there is any deficiency, it is from me.

I hope you will allow this voice to reach a reader who yearns for a noble reconciliation between science and faith, and to remember that the universe surrounding us is not silent or cold, but alive‚Äîglorifying, submitting, and humbling itself‚Äîand that the human being is charged with listening well.

With gratitude for your time and attention,

Your brother

Dr. Jameel Al-Saqqayya
Consultant Anesthesiologist at Ministry of Health Hospitals, Saudi Arabia

‚öñÔ∏è Limits of Inference: A Methodological Statement

When you begin reading these cosmic hymns, you enter a world of contemplation and research where the Qur'anic text intersects with scientific discovery, and spiritual enlightenment with empirical investigation. To ensure the clarity of the epistemological journey and the purity of the scientific endeavor, we place before you this necessary clarification:

üéØ First: On the Nature of Purpose and Method

The essential purpose of this work is to contemplate the meanings of the Noble Revelation regarding the universe's glorification, submission, and humility before its Creator, the Almighty‚Äînot to claim the construction of a conclusive empirical proof of "non-human consciousness" in the narrow scientific sense. The book is an attempt to explore those vast horizons where the truths of faith meet the achievements of science, without mixing their levels or equating their differing meanings.

üìä Second: On Handling Scientific Material

All contemporary scientific studies and theories mentioned in this book are presented for consideration and illumination, not for conclusive argument or decisive proof. The realities of the unseen are established by the text of the Clear Revelation, and we do not burden empirical results‚Äîno matter how precise‚Äîwith metaphysical meanings that exceed their methodological boundaries. I have reviewed hundreds of peer-reviewed scientific journals and have been careful to present multiple viewpoints on controversial issues, without bias toward any particular school.

üß† Third: On Distinguishing Levels of Consciousness

We distinguish here with methodological clarity between "consciousness" in its religious, faith-based meaning‚Äîindicated by creatures' glorification, prostration, and submission to their Creator, from the category of transmitted knowledge (sam'iyyƒÅt) that we believe in as it came‚Äîand "consciousness" in its operational scientific meaning, consisting of perceptual states, information processing, and neural connections, which belongs to the category of measurement subject to review and development. There is no equation between the two levels, nor confusion of their distinct meanings in their respective fields.

üî¨ Fourth: On Theoretical Frameworks and Scientific Hypotheses

Frameworks and theoretical models‚Äîsuch as Integrated Information Theory, philosophical visions like panpsychism, and some quantum interpretations of consciousness‚Äîare mentioned as hypotheses and interpretive frameworks subject to ongoing debate and development, not as established facts or subjects of scientific consensus. What among them is weak in replicability or outside rigorous scientific peer review is not taken as evidence, and if mentioned, it is in a critical spirit, not evidential.

‚úçÔ∏è Fifth: On Style and Method

The aesthetic and rhetorical style used to serve the faith-based and contemplative purpose does not stand in place of scientific proof. Where an empirical claim is mentioned, it is formulated in specific language attributed to its original sources. This book is "an attempt to gather spiritual experiences and scientific facts and connect them with the Qur'anic text and prophetic texts," not a declaration of categorical truths in matters of interpretation.

üîÑ Sixth: On the Nature of Review and Development

Given the renewable nature of research, some scientific data may change with the advancement of knowledge, and unintentional oversight may occur in documenting a reference or completing its details. Therefore, the author commits to addressing any deficiency or error in subsequent editions once brought to attention, apologizes in advance for any unintended confusion, and welcomes specialists' observations to develop the work and improve its documentation.

‚ö†Ô∏è Seventh: On Use and Responsibility

This book is directed toward intellectual contemplation and epistemological dialogue, "an attempt to awaken questioning" as I expressed in its introduction, and does not represent professional scientific, academic, or religious consultation, nor does any institutional party assume responsibility for it or for its reader. Any use of its content outside its contemplative context is the sole responsibility of its user.

üåü Finally: On Understanding Comparisons and Meanings

Comparisons between the meanings of revelation and some empirical results are understood as approximations in reference, not identities in meaning. The truth of the universe's glorification, submission, and humility remains established by the text of the Noble Revelation; we comprehend what God has permitted, and we entrust what lies beyond to His knowledge, Glory be to Him.

These are hymns for contemplation, a symphony for reflection, and an invitation to perceive the beauty of creation in its glorification of its Great Creator.
