I: The Dance of Consciousness in the Theater of Atoms

In the depths of existence, where particles of matter glow and electrons dance in their orbits with wondrous lightness, a timeless question has haunted scientists and philosophers alike: Could it be that consciousness, that deep sense of being, is embedded in the very core of the atomic and quantum world?

Classical science has long explained consciousness as the result of complex interactions between brain neurons. But delving into the quantum world, with its paradoxes and oddities, has revealed phenomena that shake our certainties: Particles that exist in more than one place at once, and atoms that communicate instantaneously across vast distances [Fundamental Principles of Quantum Mechanics, American Institute of Physics].

Consciousness and quantum: Collision or harmony?

That inner feeling that characterizes every human being - the ability to feel, perceive, and reflect on oneself - seems to be beyond any purely materialistic explanation. How can rigid physical structures, atoms and molecules, produce subjective experience? This is exactly where what is known in contemporary philosophy as the "hard problem of consciousness" arises.

In 1995, Australian philosopher David Chalmers coined the term in his famous article "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness" published in the Journal of Consciousness Studies, where he distinguished between the "easy" (describing brain functions) and the "hard" (explaining how subjective experience arises).

II: Scientific hypotheses about quantum consciousness:

With the development of quantum physics - the branch that studies the behavior of tiny, invisible particles - new hypotheses are emerging that suggest that "consciousness may not be a classical product of the brain, but an intrinsically quantum phenomenon".

A. The Quantum Mind Hypothesis (Orch-OR)
In this theory, proposed by Stuart Hameroff and Roger Penrose, it is hypothesized that quantum processes occur within the microtubules of neurons that produce the experience of consciousness. The hypothesis is known as "Orchestrated Objective Reduction (Orch OR)" and its most prominent details were published in a 2014 paper in the journal Physics of Life Reviews.
B. The Two-Face Hypothesis
This theory is "essentially philosophical" and has its roots in the works of Spinoza and Schelling, and has been re-adopted by a number of contemporary thinkers such as Harald Atmanshaper. It states that "matter and consciousness are two sides of one irreducible reality" just as "light" can be treated as a wave or a particle in physics.

C. Criticism of physicists
Despite the philosophical and scientific appeal of these theories, most of the physics community is "highly skeptical". In his 2000 article "The Myth of Quantum Consciousness" published in The Humanist, physicist Victor Stenger described quantum consciousness as a scientific myth that is not based on experimentation or proof.

Christoph Koch, director of the Allen Institute for Brain Research, in his book The Quest for Consciousness: A Neurobiological Approach (2004) also expressed skepticism: "The brain is a very humid and warm environment, making it unlikely that subtle quantum phenomena can persist there without being collapsed by heat and biological noise."

While scientists and philosophers are divided on the quantum basis of consciousness, there seems to be a tacit consensus that our consciousness is the most complex in existence. Whether it is the result of complex molecular interactions, or a cosmic entanglement that transcends time and space, it remains the most intractable mystery of 21st century science.

Perhaps these complexities do not mean a clash between quantum and consciousness, but rather that quantum opens a new window to understand the self, not just as a neural machine, but as a being that interacts with the universe on a deeper, more subtle, and perhaps more "spiritual" level than we thought.

III: The Double Slit Experiment: When observation changes reality

To understand the possible relationship between consciousness and the quantum world, it is necessary to look at one of the strangest and most important experiments in the history of physics: The double-slit experiment. This experiment reveals that what we know about reality can change just by observing it.

What does the experiment say?

Imagine that we shoot electrons - tiny particles that are part of an atom - toward a barrier with two small slits. Behind the barrier is a screen that records where the electrons arrive.
When we don't watch the electrons pass through the slits, the screen shows an interference pattern, similar to what happens when a wave of water passes through two holes and interferes. This means that the electron is behaving like a wave, and it looks like it's passing through both slits at the same time!

However, when we measure or observe the path the electron takes, the interference pattern suddenly disappears, and instead a pattern that resembles the behavior of ordinary particles appears - as if the electron has chosen to pass through only one slit.

As the famous physicist Richard Feynman said in his lectures: "This experiment contains the core of the whole puzzle of quantum mechanics."
(The Feynman Lectures on Physics, Vol. 3, 1965).

Does consciousness affect matter?

This puzzling phenomenon raises a big question: Does our mere observation change what is happening? Does human consciousness play a role in shaping reality?

Copenhagen Interpretation: No need for consciousness
The traditional explanation for this phenomenon is known as the Copenhagen Interpretation, put forward in the early 20th century by scientists such as Niels Bohr and Heisenberg. According to this interpretation:

  • A quantum system (such as an electron) exists in a "potential" state before measurement.
  • The process of measurement is what "forces" the system to choose a specific state (passing through the left or right slit, for example).
But the observer does not have to be a conscious human being. The mere presence of a physical interaction (a device that records data) is enough to turn probability into reality.

Observer consciousness: Does it play a role?
Some scientists have questioned the adequacy of this explanation. Nobel Prize-winning physicist Eugene Wigner asked in his famous paper "Remarks on the Mind-Body Question" (1961): Could it be that the consciousness of the observer himself - not just the apparatus - is the decisive factor that makes one of the possibilities a reality?

To provoke discussion, imagine a hypothetical experiment known as Wegener's Friend: What if there is one person inside a room conducting the experiment, and another outside who does not yet know the result? Does reality collapse for the inside observer before the outside? Does the electron remain in a state of "possibility" until one of them informs the other? These questions continue to spark philosophical and scientific debate to this day.

From Quantum Physics to the Depths of the Self
The double-slit experiment shows that observation changes the physical behavior of tiny particles. The traditional explanation is that physical interaction is enough to explain this, with no need to introduce consciousness. But some argue that consciousness may be more than a bystander - it may be an active element in "choosing reality". This experiment doesn't just tell us about electrons... It opens the door to major existential questions: Is the universe waiting for us to notice it until it manifests? Is our consciousness connected to the deepest layers of reality?

IV: Quantum Entanglement: Hidden connections between minds?

Among the most complex phenomena of modern physics, quantum entanglement stands out as one of the most surprising and philosophical questions. According to this phenomenon, two entangled particles remain strangely connected: As soon as we measure the state of one, the state of the other is immediately determined, regardless of the distance between them. This is what Albert Einstein sarcastically described as "spooky action at a distance" in his critique of the interpretation of quantum mechanics.

This phenomenon was demonstrated in the Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen (EPR) experiment in 1935, which aimed to highlight what Einstein considered to be the "shortcomings of quantum mechanics" in that it did not provide a complete description of physical reality.

What is quantum entanglement?

Quantum Entanglement is a physical phenomenon that occurs when subatomic particles (such as photons or electrons) interact in such a way that the state of each particle is correlated with the other, regardless of the distance between them. In other words, when we measure a certain property of the first particle (such as spin), we immediately know the corresponding property of the second particle, even if it is at the other end of the universe.

Crucial experiments that proved quantum entanglement
  • John Clauser and Stuart Friedman Experiment (1972): The first practical experiment to test Bell's Inequality. Proved that correlated particles communicate instantaneously, bypassing the speed limit imposed by special relativity.
  • The Alain Aspey Experiment (1982): Confirmed correlations beyond what classical theories allow, supporting the predictions of quantum mechanics and Local Realism. Aspeh was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2022.
  • Anton Zeilinger's Team Experiment (1998): Used coherent photons sent over 400 meters via fiber optic cables. Showed instant correlations, confirming the "non-locality" of the universe. Zelinger was also awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2022.
  • China's Mesius Satellite Experiment (2017): Tested quantum entanglement at astronomical distances (1,200 km), proving it persists over vast distances.
  • A recent study published in Nature in 2023: Confirmed entanglement between particles separated by large distances with ultra-precise measurements, showing the effect was faster than the speed of light.

Toward a deeper understanding of cosmic consciousness

Quantum entanglement experiments provide scientific evidence of a phenomenon that challenges our traditional understanding of reality and opens the door to new insights into the nature of consciousness and existence.

In his book Mindful Universe: Quantum Mechanics and the Common Observer, Henry Stapp discusses how quantum mechanics, especially the phenomenon of quantum entanglement, suggests that the universe is not just an assembly of separate parts, but an interconnected system where events in one part affect other parts, regardless of the distance between them. "The quantum universe is an interconnected whole, where measurements in one place lead to instantaneous changes in the quantum state on a large scale," Stapp explains.

V: From Electrons to Minds: Is there entanglement?

While quantum mechanics confirms these phenomena at the subatomic level, some scientists are beginning to ask a bold question: Could similar entanglement occur within the human brain? Could this explain some features of consciousness such as unity of perception or even "telepathy"?

In the Journal of Neuroscience, Tononi and Edelman (1998) wondered how the brain produces a unified experience of consciousness, even though it consists of billions of neurons. They suggested that the unity of consciousness may be the product of deeply interconnected mechanisms, perhaps not limited to electrical connections alone.

New hypotheses: Entanglement in our neurons?
In their study, titled "Generation of Entangled Biphoton Pairs in the Myelin Shell", the lipid shell called myelin, which envelops the axons of neurons, may provide a suitable environment for the generation of quantum entangled photon pairs via the interaction of carbon and hydrogen atoms.

This hypothesis - though in need of stronger experimental support - is gaining some traction as biological research has uncovered quantum mechanisms demonstrated in living organisms:

  • Photosynthesis in plants: In a study published in Nature in 2007 led by Fleming et al, quantum efficiency in energy transfer within photosynthetic centers was revealed.
  • The bio-compass of migratory birds: A study in Nature Communications in 2016 showed that birds use a mechanism based on quantum interactions between electrons to determine directions.

Between consciousness and entanglement: Hypothesis or discovery?
Could it be that there is an entanglement between human minds or even between the human mind and the universe? Although these questions have yet to be definitively answered, quantum physics is paving the way for a new phase of scientific thinking - where matter and consciousness are no longer two separate entities, but a single network that interacts across levels we do not yet recognize.

How does quantum entanglement happen?
Although experiments have succeeded in proving the existence of quantum entanglement, how it occurs is still the subject of deep philosophical and scientific debate. There are several explanations:

  1. The traditional Copenhagen interpretation: Particles do not have specific properties prior to measurement.
  2. The Many Worlds Theory: Every possible outcome occurs in a parallel universe.
  3. David Bohm's Theory of Implicit Order: Bohm sees the entire universe as an "undivided whole". What we see as separate particles are just surface manifestations of a deeper, connected reality.

In his book "Wholeness and the Implicate Order", Bohm states:
"At the deepest level, the universe is an undivided whole, an interconnected system that cannot be divided into independent parts. Each part of the universe 'folds' within itself the entire universe, just as each part of a hologram contains information about the whole."

VII: The miraculous design within the atom

For approximation: If an atom were the size of a football field, its nucleus would be the size of a small pea at its center. As small as it is, that nucleus contains more than 99.9% of the atom's mass. Electrons orbit around it in near-empty space. This enormous percentage of "vacuum" within the atom is what makes matter compressible, and explains how, at the microscopic level, most of the universe is an "organized vacuum".

But the real miracle lies in the delicate balance of forces within the atom: The strong nuclear force that holds protons and neutrons together inside the nucleus must be very carefully tuned. If this force were even a tiny bit stronger, heavy atoms would quickly collapse. If it were weaker, nuclei would not form at all. This is what is known as the "fine-tuning" of the universe.

In his book The Goldilocks Enigma, renowned physicist Paul Davies comments on this phenomenon:
"The universe is amazingly fine-tuned to host life... If the laws of physics were even slightly different, there might not be galaxies, stars, planets, atoms, or any of the basic components of life."

I: The Conscious Behavior of the Cell

On a quiet night, as I contemplated images of mitochondria under an electron microscope, I was struck by an astonishment that I remember to this day. How could these microscopic structures produce so much energy more efficiently than our most complex factories?

ATP synthase: The Amazing Molecular Machine
In 1997, Professor Paul Boyer, Nobel Laureate in Chemistry, devoted an entire paper to this enzyme, and gave it a telling title: "ATP synthase - a marvelous molecular machine". The enzyme acts like a nanoscale rotary engine, spinning at up to 150 revolutions per second, producing three ATP molecules with each full revolution! Imagine a car engine the size of your thumb that runs at over 90% efficiency.

Irreducible complexity: A scientific challenge
Professor Michael Behe describes in his book Darwin's Black Box: "Irreducible complexity is a single system made up of several interacting, coordinated parts that contribute to an essential function, where the removal of any one of the parts causes the system to effectively cease functioning."

The cell regulates traffic within the cell
Inside the cell, there is a transportation system more complex than an entire city's transportation network. Scientists from the 2013 Nobel Prize in Medicine and Physiology discovered the mechanisms that regulate the movement of vesicles within the cell. "The cell possesses an elaborate internal transportation system... This mechanism operates with a precision and timeliness that surpasses the most sophisticated traffic systems in the world."

Cells that commit suicide so as not to harm the body
One of the most surprising phenomena in the world of cells is called apoptosis, where a cell - when it becomes diseased or harmful to the body - activates a self-suicide mechanism! When a cell is damaged or infected, it triggers a complex internal mechanism that leads to its own self-destruction. It's like saying "my life for the safety of the whole body"!

Dr. Harris Perlman of Stanford University says: "What we're seeing here is a self-sacrifice system programmed with amazing precision. The cell is saying: 'If I can't do my job properly, I'd better remove myself before I cause harm to the whole organism.'"

"We will show them Our signs in the horizons and in themselves until it becomes clear to them that it is the truth"
[Faslat: 53]

From the infinitesimal atom to the sprawling galaxy, the universe plays a harmonious symphony of praise and reverence for the Almighty. Perhaps the greatest lesson we learn from this journey is to join - consciously and willingly - in this renewed cosmic symphony, and to make our whole life a praise to God.

"The seven heavens, the earth, and those who are in them praise Him, and there is nothing but praise in His praise, but you do not understand their praise, for He was wise and forgiving"
[Al-Israa'a: 44].

And Allah knows best and is the wisest.
